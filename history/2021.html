Married to Singapore:
What are migrant wives saying ‘I do’ to?

Rexanne Yap Ray Shinh
Chan Yong Zhi Joel
Mah Yun Yi, Jeanne
Wun Yun Fang
Top Final-Year Project (Journalism)


Click here to view FYP repository

During the circuit breaker period in 2020, families were cooped up at home to prevent the transmission of COVID-19. While some found it as a break from daily life, an invisible group found themselves trapped round the clock with their abusers.

The Association of Women for Action and Research released a report in July last year, shedding light on a particularly vulnerable segment of Singapore's population - migrant wives - who had made more distress calls to the advocacy group during this period.

Migrant wives married to Singaporean men make up a substantial portion of Singapore’s population. In 2019, one in four marriages was between a Singaporean groom and a migrant wife. Most hail from developing countries in Asia, and tend to come from a lower socio-economic background. Leaving their motherland behind, they face a lack of social ties, little familial support, and culture shock as they struggle to belong in their new home country.

Many of the women are first placed on Short-Term or Long-Term Visit Passes before they can apply for Permanent Residency. These passes make them dependent on their spouses for housing, income, and social security. These dependencies exacerbate the power imbalance in the marriage, allowing Singaporean husbands to exploit them in abuse cases.

Due to their foreigner status, foreign wives are rejected from state benefits and assistance even after they have sought help. Though they work here, marry our men, and raise Singaporean children, why does their status continue to exclude them from the Singapore core?

The team explores what it means for migrant wives when they marry to Singapore.

So Far Yet So Near:
Exploring Empathy in Virtual Reality

Lee Hui Min
Aw Yao Feng
Winnie Goh Wen Pin
Samuel Lim Weng Leong
Top Final-Year Project (Research)


Click here to view FYP repository

“So Far Yet So Near” explores the theoretical mechanisms behind virtual reality's (VR) ability to induce empathy and prosocial behaviour by proposing a conceptual framework based on Construal Level Theory (CLT). It posits that increased perceptions of virtual presence, together with a reduction of perceived psychological distance, may translate into prosocial behaviours such as intention to donate money and volunteer.

Between These Bones

Pranamika Subhalaxmi Shridhar
Caitlin Elizebeth Park
Joyce Ng Hui Jie
Li Jiaqi
Top Final-Year Project (Broadcast)


Click here to view FYP repository

Singapore: a bustling metropolitan city-state. In a staggeringly short timespan, it has become a ‘first world’ nation despite its geographical and historical limitations. But what does this mean to the people who live within her borders?

An intimate look at Singapore as a body, "Between These Bones" is a poetic exploration of the everyday lives of people in the country. The film looks at how they cope with the inherent limitations presented in spaces within the country and how they attempt to transcend them.

Divided into three chapters, each segment explores the effects of space on different individuals. The documentary considers how a space, despite being heavily controlled, is ultimately defined by the people who live in them.

Genesis

Bryan Tan Yong Ern (CS'22), Fontanilla Base Eryka Joyce (CS'22), Ou Jie Hui Joy (CS'22), Tan YanYing Nikki (CS'22)
Esthétique Award Recipient
Previous Next
Click here to view work

In examining the layers and nuances of self, we question our identity and get lost in the whirlwind of personas we put forward. It seems as though a different version of ourselves exists for different circumstances and thus, we perform to conform. But with each performance we bring to the surface a piece of ourselves, and they all come together to form a beautiful whole.

With the film touching on the personal subject of individuality, the film mainly capitalizes on close-ups of the dancer to create a sense of intimacy between the audience and the subject. This includes shots of her neck, arms, and legs as she moves. The film also aims to use the vibrancy (or lack thereof) of the different locations to capture the subject’s mental state. For instance, the slowness and darkness of the underwater shots will be used to convey uncertainty and the feeling of being stifled, whereas the brightness and openness of the garden reflects a sense of rebirth and renewal. While none of us have truly attained the self-assuredness we seek, this film shows you what we think it feels like.

Genesis is a performative documentary on the journey of self-discovery, interpreted through dance.

WeeVolunteer AY20/21

Service Award Recipient
Previous Next

In spite of the COVID-19 pandemic, WeeVolunteer (WeeVo) was able to adapt and provide WKWSCI students and staff with opportunities to give back to the community locally and abroad.

Locally, instead of physical volunteering, WeeVo conducted Zoom sessions for volunteers to interact with our beneficiaries at MINDS@Napiri every weekend, and also initiated letter writing sessions, pre-recorded volunteer sessions, and other remote opportunities for the elderly residents and our partnering organisations.

Internationally, WeeVo conducted workshops for struggling businesses and students in Laos to upgrade their digital marketing skills, to help them cope amidst the COVID-19 crisis. On top of that, they conducted a social campaign in partnership with DoTheKindness, to aid the girls under the care of the business owner.

In between these programmes, WeeVo conducted a fundraiser for the Beirut crisis, along with donation drives and canvassing efforts in order to better provide assistance to their beneficiaries. A new volunteer wing was also founded, and was launched by introducing Cleaner's Appreciation Day in WKWSCI, as well as a Beach Cleanup event to remind people to be more environmentally conscious.

In spite of the circumstances through the year, WeeVo made itself adaptable to the situation and was still able to give back to the various communities in Singapore, as well as those abroad. Their dedication to serving the various communities in need of aid through the ups and downs of the COVID-19 crisis exemplifies why they are deserving of this nomination.

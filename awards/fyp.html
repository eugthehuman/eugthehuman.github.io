Final-Year Project provides students with the opportunity to showcase the abilities and talents they have acquired during their time at WKWSCI. Since its inception, one of the goals of the WKWSCI Final-Year Project is for students to complete a project that reaches a wider audience than the school. WKWSCI students do campaigns for clients that reach broad audiences. They produce videos that are shown at international festivals and win international competitions. They produce impactful journalism that is published in mainstream media. Our students have a remarkable record of completing research projects that are presented at international conferences and published in international, peer-reviewed academic journals.

The Top FYP Awards are awarded to fourth-year undergraduates who have submitted the most impressive final-year projects under the respective categories: Journalism, Campaign, Research and Broadcast.

Married to Singapore:
What are migrant wives saying ‘I do’ to?

Rexanne Yap Ray Shinh
Chan Yong Zhi Joel
Mah Yun Yi, Jeanne
Wun Yun Fang
Top Final-Year Project (Journalism)


Click here to view FYP repository

During the circuit breaker period in 2020, families were cooped up at home to prevent the transmission of COVID-19. While some found it as a break from daily life, an invisible group found themselves trapped round the clock with their abusers.

The Association of Women for Action and Research released a report in July last year, shedding light on a particularly vulnerable segment of Singapore's population - migrant wives - who had made more distress calls to the advocacy group during this period.

Migrant wives married to Singaporean men make up a substantial portion of Singapore’s population. In 2019, one in four marriages was between a Singaporean groom and a migrant wife. Most hail from developing countries in Asia, and tend to come from a lower socio-economic background. Leaving their motherland behind, they face a lack of social ties, little familial support, and culture shock as they struggle to belong in their new home country.

Many of the women are first placed on Short-Term or Long-Term Visit Passes before they can apply for Permanent Residency. These passes make them dependent on their spouses for housing, income, and social security. These dependencies exacerbate the power imbalance in the marriage, allowing Singaporean husbands to exploit them in abuse cases.

Due to their foreigner status, foreign wives are rejected from state benefits and assistance even after they have sought help. Though they work here, marry our men, and raise Singaporean children, why does their status continue to exclude them from the Singapore core?

The team explores what it means for migrant wives when they marry to Singapore.

How Are You, Really?:

“How Are You, Really?” is a social campaign founded on the need for youths to check in with close others around them by engaging in deeper conversations about mental health. “How Are You, Really?” seeks to equip youths with the knowledge and skills to empower them to support those with depression.

So Far Yet So Near:
Exploring Empathy in Virtual Reality

Lee Hui Min
Aw Yao Feng
Winnie Goh Wen Pin
Samuel Lim Weng Leong
Top Final-Year Project (Research)


Click here to view FYP repository

“So Far Yet So Near” explores the theoretical mechanisms behind virtual reality's (VR) ability to induce empathy and prosocial behaviour by proposing a conceptual framework based on Construal Level Theory (CLT). It posits that increased perceptions of virtual presence, together with a reduction of perceived psychological distance, may translate into prosocial behaviours such as intention to donate money and volunteer.

Between These Bones

Pranamika Subhalaxmi Shridhar
Caitlin Elizebeth Park
Joyce Ng Hui Jie
Li Jiaqi
Top Final-Year Project (Broadcast)


Click here to view FYP repository

Singapore: a bustling metropolitan city-state. In a staggeringly short timespan, it has become a ‘first world’ nation despite its geographical and historical limitations. But what does this mean to the people who live within her borders?

An intimate look at Singapore as a body, "Between These Bones" is a poetic exploration of the everyday lives of people in the country. The film looks at how they cope with the inherent limitations presented in spaces within the country and how they attempt to transcend them.

Divided into three chapters, each segment explores the effects of space on different individuals. The documentary considers how a space, despite being heavily controlled, is ultimately defined by the people who live in them.
